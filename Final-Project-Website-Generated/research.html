Understanding colon cancer biology and how gene expression is measured.

Why Does This Matter?

Colorectal cancer is the third most common cancer worldwide and the second leading cause of cancer death. Early-stage disease may produce few or no symptoms, so early detection generally provides more treatment options.

Leading cause of cancer death

  • Age is the main risk factor, although younger people can also be affected.
  • A family history of colorectal cancer increases risk.
  • Diet, obesity, and physical inactivity are associated with higher risk.
  • Early-stage disease may produce few or no symptoms.
  • Changes in bowel habits and blood in stool are common warning signs.
  • General symptoms can include abdominal discomfort, fatigue, and iron deficiency.
  • Standard screening options include stool-based tests, colonoscopy, sigmoidoscopy, stool DNA testing, and CT colonography.
  • Early detection generally provides more treatment options and better outcomes.
  • Treatment depends on disease location and stage and may include surgery, chemotherapy, targeted therapy, immunotherapy, or radiation.

From mutations to tumor development

Colon cancer develops when genetic and epigenetic changes disrupt the systems that normally control cell division, DNA repair, and programmed cell death. These changes can alter gene expression, causing some genes to produce too much mRNA and others too little. The resulting changes in protein levels can allow damaged cells to survive, divide, and form tumors.

Gene-expression analysis identifies changes associated with tumor tissue, but it does not by itself prove causation. It is useful for studying tumor biology and identifying candidate biomarkers or pathways, not as a replacement for standard colorectal-cancer screening.

What is gene expression?

Most cells in the human body contain nearly the same DNA, but different cells express different genes, allowing them to perform specialized functions.

How do we measure gene expression?

A DNA microarray is a laboratory technology that measures the expression levels of thousands of known genes in the same experiment. The chip contains thousands of DNA probes. Labeled molecules prepared from the sample bind to complementary probes, and the measured signal provides an estimate of how much corresponding RNA was present.

Collect biological samples

Healthy and tumor tissue samples are collected so their gene activity can be compared.

Microarray signal pattern

By comparing expression values between healthy and tumor samples, researchers can identify genes that are expressed at higher or lower levels in cancer. This may help identify candidate biomarkers, genes and pathways associated with cancer biology, and directions for future therapeutic research.

Research Question

Which genes are expressed differently in colon cancer compared with healthy colon tissue?

To answer this question, we analyze a public DNA microarray dataset and statistically compare gene-expression values from healthy and tumor samples.
